Accessibility

 

As many people as possible

 

Interacting with a laptop or smartphone involves the senses of touch, sight, and hearing. One billion people, or 15% of the world’s population, experience disabilities that can influence this interaction.

 

Accessibility testing

Accessibility testing is part of usability testing. It allows us to collect information about how the application is used by people with disabilities. Its purpose is to make the internet accessible for as many people as possible.

This type of software testing is focused on making the content usable for differently-abled people, people who lack technical skills and equipment knowledge, new users, or just people with limitations caused by age. Accessibility should be tested technically, but also by the users.

 

The web accessibility checklist focuses on:

  • Image alt text:

People with low vision use screen readers to “see” what’s on the page. To make it usable for them, you must include alternative text for all images and non-text elements on the page.

  • Page titles:

Each page should have a short descriptive title so the person knows what’s on the page.

  • Text resizing:

Make sure that people can make the text size on your page larger.

  • Keyboard navigation:

People with dexterity issues use voice-controlled navigation or arrow keys instead of a mouse to navigate websites. Make sure your content is designed to be operated in this way.

  • Color contrast:

Make sure that there is enough contrast between the text and the background of the page content.

  • Flashing and blinking content elements:

Ensure that any web content flashes or blinks slow enough speed to avoid triggering seizures.

  • Video and audio content:

Include captions or text transcripts for your video content. There should also be audio or text descriptions of what’s happening in a video.

 

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines are part of a series of web accessibility guidelines. WCAG was published by the Web Accessibility Initiative of the World Wide Web Consortium (the international standards organization for the Internet).

 

According to WCAG, there are four principles of accessibility:

  • perceivable

available through sight, hearing, or touch.

  • operable

compatible with keyboard or mouse.

  • understandable

easy to comprehend.

  • robust

content must be interpreted reliably by assistive technologies.
