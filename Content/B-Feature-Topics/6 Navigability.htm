Navigability

Every step you take

 

A navigation test is a tool used to understand what is confusing your users. It also allows to get feedback It shows how users go through the website or application. The results help to find critical user flows, and improve your information architecture.

To test navigability you need to upload a series of screens. You also have to give testers a brief scenario, for example, "Imagine you're looking for a Christmas gift for your nephew and find this website through a Google search."  You present your interface to the user and ask them to complete a task. At each step, the position and timing of the clicks are recorded. If the user clicks within one of the hit zones, they go to the next step. You follow the user’s way through the interface, which can be clicked to navigate to the next step.

If they don't click within a hit zone (weak spot), the test ends. That we can ask for feedback on why they chose to click just there. This type of test shows how many users successfully clicked in the correct place at each step and where people dropped off.

‍Navigation testing is the best way to compare the performance of an original design to an improved version.

What we have to remember is to keep the instructions clear and simple. Complexity will lead your testers to click in unexpected places.

 

To optimize the user experience of navigating through your website we have to think about:

  • Menu options

Do the menu choices guide users as quickly as possible to the destination point? Are there too many or too few options?

  • Mental models

Are you meeting users’ expectations of how the site should work?

  • Icons and labels

Are the symbols and terms understandable or do they need further explanation or translation?

  • Information overload

Make sure that you provided the information users need to use your site. Too much information or too little information can be frustrating.

  • Regulated attention

Don’t make users think or work too hard. The risk is - that they will leave.

  • Menu visibility

Is the menu for key activities visible or hidden?

  • Clear menu, button labels

Provide one or two entry points based on clear menu or button labels. In this case, less is more.

  • Navigation steps

How many steps do users have to go through to get to the point? Are there any steps that slow them down? Can they be eliminated?

  • Navigation design pattern

Does the navigation design (tabs, menus, accordions, carousels, etc.) help users to find what they need? If not - redesign.

  • Search function & filters

Are users able to find what they are looking for using a search function?

 

First click testing

Your users’ first click is their most important. First click testing is a method for measuring the usability of a website, app, or design by finding out how easy it is to complete a given task.

In a first click test, the participant is given a task (e.g. “Where would you click to buy this product?”) and is then shown an image of an interface, on which they click to complete the task.

The position of their click is recorded, along with how long they took to click. We can also ask the user to explain why they clicked where they did. First click tests can be run using screenshots, sketches, wireframes, prototypes, and mock-ups. Using it we can test all the design processes.
